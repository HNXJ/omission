# Results: Phase 1 - Hierarchical Omission Responses

## 📊 Widespread Neural Representation of Predicted Absence
We first interrogated whether the absence of a predicted stimulus (Omission) triggers a distinct neural signature. Population-averaged PSTHs revealed robust neural activity locked to the expected time of the missing stimulus (FIG_03). Surprisingly, these "ghost signals" were not restricted to high-order areas but were observed across the entire 11-area hierarchy, from V1 to PFC.

## 📊 Hierarchical Scaling of Surprise Magnitude
While omission responses were widespread, their magnitude scaled significantly with hierarchical depth. Using a normalized Omission Index ($OI$), we observed that higher cortical areas (PFC, FEF) exhibited significantly stronger modulation compared to primary visual areas ($p < 1e-4$, FIG_03B). This scaling aligns with the "Predictive Routing" framework, where high-order areas integrate long-range predictions and generate robust error signals when those predictions are violated.

## 📊 Identification of High-Fidelity Omission Units
At the single-unit level, we identified a sub-population of units that responded specifically to the omission event while remaining relatively quiescent during standard stimulus presentation. These "ghost neurons" maintain precise temporal alignment to the expected stimulus onset (FIG_04). The discovery of these units across 842 Stable-Plus neurons provides strong evidence for a discrete neural manifold dedicated to representing violated expectations.
