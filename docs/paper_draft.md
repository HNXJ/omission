# Omission halts local field potential structure and reshapes spiking across the cortical hierarchy

Hamed Nejat, Dept. of Psychology, Vanderbilt University
Advisor: Dr. Andre Bastos, Dept. of Psychology, Vanderbilt University; Vanderbilt Brain Institute

## Abstract
Predictive coding proposes that the brain continuously compares expected input with actual input and tries to minimize the mismatch between them. In neurophysiology, the predictive routing framework proposes that this process is expressed through oscillatory dynamics across the cortical hierarchy (V1, V2, V3d, V3a, V4, MT, MST, TEO, FST, FEF, PFC). In that view, stronger low-frequency alpha and beta activity is linked to predictable input context, while unexpected input is linked to stronger gamma activity and spiking. Earlier work proposed that these rhythms control the neuronal communications by suppressing or allowing feedforward signals, rather than by sending a separate abstract prediction-error code. In that case, making a prediction-error context without the feedforward sensory signal being present, should be reflected in alpha-beta bands. Here we tested what happens when an expected visual input is not delivered. To resemble the context of prediction-error without a visual input, we used a visual omission task with repeated four-item sequences and recorded spiking activity and local field potentials (LFPs) across lower and higher visual and frontal areas (V1-PFC). We asked whether omission acts like a strong sensory surprise in both spikes and LFP, and whether omission changes cortical spectral structure in a way that fits predictive routing. We found that omission does not act like a normal sensory input. Spiking responses to omission were sparse and were strongest in higher-order areas, while lower-order sensory cortex (V1-V2) showed little omission-driven multi-unit spiking. At the same time, omission caused broad low-frequency LFP changes across the entire hierarchy, with strong effects in theta, alpha, and beta and much weaker effects in gamma. These results suggest that omission mainly disrupts the expected low-frequency cortical state, while only a small set of neurons shows clear omission-linked spiking.

## Methods
Neural data were acquired from multi-area, dense laminar macaque electrophysiology experiments (V1, V2, V3d, V3a, V4, MT, MST, TEO, FST, FEF, and PFC). High-density laminar probes were targeted to these areas, with session-specific assignments resolved via high-resolution histological reconstruction cross-referenced with repository mapping tables. 

To ensure precise spatial assignment, we implemented a hierarchical channel mapping strategy. When a single probe spanned multiple areas, the channel axis was partitioned into contiguous segments based on cortical thickness and laminar boundaries. This was rigidly enforced: LFP and MUAe were analyzed according to these partitions, while spike-sorted units were assigned to area based on their peak-firing-rate channel. Area assignments strictly followed the hierarchical order: V1 -> V2 -> V3d/a -> V4 -> MT/MST -> TEO/FST -> FEF -> PFC. The alias rule `DP -> V4` was strictly applied, and V3 labels were resolved into V3d and V3a.

## Results
Omission-sensitive units are a selective minority across the 11-area cortical hierarchy (V1, V2, V3d, V3a, V4, MT, MST, TEO, FST, FEF, PFC). While stimulus-modulated populations show robust sensory activity, most units are unresponsive to omissions. Sparsely distributed omission-positive and omission-negative groups exhibit firing changes strictly locked to missing-item intervals. Primary sensory areas (V1, V2) exhibit negligible omission-linked spiking, whereas higher-order areas (TEO, FST, FEF, PFC) exhibit higher counts of O+ and O- motifs. This functional dissociation across the hierarchy suggests that spiking responses to omission are not simple sensory error signals, but late, selective hierarchical computations. 

Omission triggers broad-spectrum changes in local field potentials across the entire hierarchy, contrasting sharply with the sparse spiking signal. Omission leads to a consistent suppression of low-frequency (Theta, Alpha, Beta) power, which is most pronounced in intermediate and high-order areas. Cross-Area CCA revealed a consistent breakdown in spectral harmony across the 11 areas during omission. Furthermore, the coupling between LFP oscillations and spiking (SFC) shifts from a stimulus-locked Gamma synchronization pattern in sensory-dominant areas (V1-V4) to a distributed Beta-structured rhythmic state in higher-order cortex (FEF, PFC).
