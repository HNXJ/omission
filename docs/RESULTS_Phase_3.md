# Results: Phase 3 - Hierarchical Signal Flow

## 📊 Feedforward Propagation of Prediction Errors
We interrogated the direction of information flow during the omission event using Spiking Granger Causality. We found a significant increase in feedforward-dominant flow from the superficial layers of lower-tier visual areas (e.g., V1) to the deep layers of higher-tier areas (e.g., PFC) during the omission window (FIG_14). This surge in feedforward influence confirms that neural omission responses behave as prediction errors that propagate up the hierarchy to update internal models.

## 📊 Feedback Gating of Internal Predictions
Complementing the feedforward surge, spectral Granger analysis revealed sustained feedback-dominant influence in the alpha/beta bands (8-25Hz) from PFC deep layers to V1 superficial layers (FIG_15). This feedback was strongest during the pre-omission period, suggesting that top-down predictions actively gate the responsiveness of lower-tier areas.

## 📊 Global Synthesis of the Omission Manifold
Synthesizing these directed influences across all 11 areas, we generated a global effective connectivity manifold (FIG_20). The manifold reveals a highly coordinated "routing state" during the omission, characterized by a rapid shift from feedback-dominant prediction maintenance to feedforward-dominant error propagation. This global reconfiguration of cortical connectivity provides the mechanism for large-scale integration of sensory surprise across the visual-prefrontal axis.
